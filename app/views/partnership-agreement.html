What a partnership agreement is

A partnership agreement states how an organisation's content team will work with other parts of the organisation, such as policy, communications or service teams. It is intended for internal use.

How a partnership agreement can help

As part of the Government Transformation Strategy all organisations have committed to “overhaul government’s legacy content and outdated publishing practices by 2020, so government services are clear, well maintained and easier to find on GOV.UK.”

Content teams can’t do this alone.

To meet users’ needs, subject matter experts (SMEs) and content teams must collaborate as early as possible in the publishing process.

Both parties’ skills are needed to express policy in language users will understand and can act on.

That means collaborating when content is first identified as being needed, not after it has been drafted and signed off.

Too often, content teams are asked to publish content without being consulted or with limited ability to make changes.

A partnership agreement can help you by setting out the principles of collaboration between content teams and SME colleagues.

How to set up a partnership agreement

Always communicate the purpose of the partnership agreement with your organisation's management and SME colleagues before work on it begins.

The 2 parties should co-create the partnership agreement together.

This means you both share ownership both of the need for the agreement and the wording it contains.

Don't present an agreement to SMEs without involving them in creating it as this could create hostility and lead to it being rejected.

How to create a partnership agreement

Run a workshop

  1. The content team presents the concept of a partnership agreement with the SMEs who create the most content.

  2. Both parties explore what they want from the agreement.

  3. The content lead then drafts an agreement based on what was discussed.

Use the ‘2-pen test’

  1. The content lead adapts a version of the partnership agreement template for their organisation.

  2. This is circulated to SMEs remotely or in a workshop.

  3. SMEs then mark up the document with a green highlighter pen for positive comments and red highlighter pen for negative comments.

  4. Through successive iterations the 2 parties arrive at an agreed text.

GDS is preparing materials which content teams can use for communication with their organisation, including a skeleton slide deck.

A partnership agreement should be succinct, written for its intended audience (for example policy or comms colleagues), and include:

  • why GOV.UK and its content matters
  • a reference to the government-wide commitment to transform government content and services
  • the principles of collaborative working towards the common goal of meeting user needs
  • the responsibilities and actions of content teams
  • the responsibilities and actions of SME colleagues

Why GOV.UK matters

Many people's main interaction with government is online. To them, GOV.UK is government.

These users are who we design and write content for. Content on GOV.UK must always meet their needs.

For every piece of content, this means knowing:

  • who it's for
  • why they need it
  • what it will enable them to do

Why content matters

Users must be able to trust and rely on GOV.UK content to find an answer, carry out a task or understand how policies affect their everyday lives.

When content is clear, concise and easy to find, they can do this without confusion. This makes government policy more effective.

It also saves departments time and money by avoiding unnecessary queries online, by phone or by post.

Our commitment to transform

As part of the the Government Transformation Strategy and Single Departmental Plans, all government departments are committed to:

Overhauling government’s legacy content and outdated publishing practices by 2020, so government services are clear, well maintained and easier to find on GOV.UK.

Managing user-focused content will help to transform the service we provide to users.

Working as one team

By working together in an open and collaborative way, we can make best use of each other’s skills and expertise.

We want to publish good content based on evidence from user research, web analytics and shared knowledge.

This means involving each other from the start - even before drafting begins.

Content teams don’t simply ‘press the publishing button’ - they plan, develop and create the best possible content for GOV.UK.

Working together in a smart and strategic way will:

  • create the right content for users and meet their needs
  • avoid wasting time drafting content that may not be published
  • hoose the best digital options and formats
  • work to agreed timelines for a smooth process
  • avoid last-minute changes and rushed decision making
  • meet everyone’s deadlines

This means we can:

  • write and publish content which is clear, concise and easy to find
  • remove content which is no longer needed
  • manage and evaluate current content so it's up to date and relevant

Our shared responsibilities

The policy team will:

  • consult with the content team before drafting, at the point when content is first planned
  • be guided by the content team’s advice on the suitability of content to meet user needs
  • provide a subject matter expert who acts as the content sponsor who is responsible for factual accuracy throughout the life of the content
  • define the user groups who are affected by the policy
  • define the user needs in terms of what actions users are expected to take
  • consult with the content team to understand how best to meet these user needs

The content team will:

  • advise on user needs, language, style, format and findability
  • ensure consistency of style and tone across GOV.UK and make content easier to find, understand and use
  • manage and evaluate the performance of published content and consult with content sponsors on appropriate changes so it is up to date, relevant and remains useful to users
  • recommend when content should be removed or withdrawn
  • manage publishing deadlines
