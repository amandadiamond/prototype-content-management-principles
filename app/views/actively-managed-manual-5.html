5. Content must be actively managed

Content must be managed throughout its lifecycle. There must be a process for regular review of guidance content to ensure it is still accurate and needed. Content should be withdrawn or unpublished where appropriate.

Content must have an owner

Content must have a named person (or persons) who is responsible for factual accuracy and content quality throughout it’s lifecycle.

The ideal situation is joint ownership of content between a subject matter expert and a content professional.

This is where the subject matter expert has ownership of the facts and is responsible for the accuracy of that content. The content professional has ownership of how those facts are written, structured and presented and is ultimately responsible for how the content is published. The joint content owners should collaborate closely to ensure the content is easily understood and meets users’ needs. Setting up a partnership agreement between the subject matter expert and the content professional can help achieve this.

Reviewing your content

Content needs continuous maintenance. If your content isn’t actively managed after publication it can become outdated, irrelevant or even be misleading to users.

If you carry on publishing new content without managing what’s already published, it will be harder for users to find what they’re looking for.

Focus your efforts on your highest priority content. This should be content that has the greatest impact for the largest number of users. Or content that forms part of your organisation’s list of top tasks.

Top tasks are the most important or most popular tasks that your users come to your content for.

Running an audit

The best way to review your content is to do an audit. There are 2 types of audit: a full audit and a rolling audit.

A full audit is usually a one-off piece of work to get a clearer picture of all the content in your content area.

You do this before doing any content improvement work and to create the foundation for an effective content management process.

Creating a rolling review

A rolling audit is a review scheduled at regular intervals as part of your ongoing content management process.

If you review 10% of your content estate each month, all your content will be audited annually.

Maintaining content this way will mean you can keep on top of the content you own, ensure it continues to meet users’ needs, is accurate and up to date.

If you don’t, content becomes unmanaged and you’ll need to carry out a full audit again.

Improving your content

Content must be correct and kept up to date. You should always make sure the latest version is published and any changes are flagged to users.

Poor quality (ineffective) content should be improved. Focus your improvement effort on your top priority content. You should regularly check your content is working for users.

Withdrawing or unpublishing content

You should withdraw content if it is not longer needed or if it’s guidance that can no longer be acted on. Users will still be able to view withdrawn content. It’ll remain on the website as a historical record.

Unpublishing content - deleting it - is not transparent and can damage the government’s reputation.

Content should only be unpublished in certain circumstances, including if it’s been published by mistake or if that content has been consolidated into another page on GOV.UK.

Content publishers must be supported

Publishers of government content must be supported within their department to manage content. This will help them ensure content meets users' needs while also being able to advocate for good content design.

Content publishers must have their content reviewed before publication. It is the responsibility of the organisation to ensure this process is in place.
