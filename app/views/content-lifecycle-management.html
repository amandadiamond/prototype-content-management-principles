Content lifecycle management

From:
Government Digital Service
Published:
20 April 2018

Why it's important to maintain content throughout its lifecycle.

Contents

  1. The lifecycle of content
  2. How to manage content
  3. Effective content lifecycle management

To meet user needs, guidance content needs to be managed from the point it is proposed through to the moment it's retired - spanning it's whole lifecycle.

It's important not to just publish and forget.

Guidance content - otherwise known as 'how-to' content that tells users how to do something - should be regularly reviewed and improved so it continues to meet a user need.

Or, it should be withdrawn or removed if it's no longer current or needed.

Content creation and management must follow the GOV.UK Content Management Principles.

The lifecycle of content

All content has a lifecycle which starts before drafting and extends beyond publishing.

Before the first draft content is even created, you and the subject matter expert must consider who it's for and why it's needed.

Good content comes from collaboration between subject matter experts and content teams.

All how-to content should have a review date to prompt the subject matter expert or content sponsor to assess if it’s still up to date and still needed.

How-to content is content that allows users to find out how to do something, as opposed to content that simply tells them what government is doing.

Examples of how-to content include guides, transactions, detailed guidance, guidance publications and forms.

Once there is no longer a need for a piece of service content, it should be withdrawn or removed.

How to manage content

Review priority content

To manage content, you first need to know how much you have and how it's performing.

Read our guidance on how to review your content.

Monitor content performance

Decide what metrics you'll use to assess your content - page views and on-page searches, for example - so you can monitor its performance over time.

The metrics you use will depend on what you expect users to do with your content. This will vary depending on the type of content.

A single metric won't give you the full picture - you'll need to monitor a combination of metrics.

Read our guidance on data and analytics.

Effective content lifecycle management

1. Change the emphasis from publishing to managing content

Place more emphasis on monitoring current content. Can you iterate and improve, rather than publish new content?

Publishing more content to fix problems with existing content means users will have difficulty finding what they're looking for.

2. Get involved earlier

For content to genuinely meet users’ needs, policy and content colleagues need to have early discussions about how the content meets those needs.

A partnership agreement between the content team and the subject matter expert can help this become the normal way of working.

3. Set up sustainable processes

Make sure you have the resources to monitor and improve your content.

There are a few methods you may want to use to monitor content performance, for example:

  • rolling reviews
  • user research
  • web metrics, such as Google analytics

4. Set priorities

Give priority to monitoring and improving content how-to content that has the biggest impact on the greatest number of users.

5. Make decisions with data

Aim to do regular user research on your most important how-to content. Underpin content decisions with analytics data.

6. Establish a content governance model

Departments must support content publishers so they can produce high quality content and follow content management best practice.

This means having the right processes in place so content can be actively managed.

All content for GOV.UK should involve the content team. For this to happen, strong content leadership is needed.

The content team must be involved from the content request and drafting stages through to publishing and post-publication management.

The content team should make sure there is a content review (or 2i) process in place and plain English and GOV.UK style are enforced.

The content team needs to establish a process for monitoring and reviewing existing content with a view to improving it.

All government content must be accessible and meet accessibility requirements.

Content should be withdrawn or removed if it no longer meets a need.
